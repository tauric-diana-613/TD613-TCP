# Anti-Selective Admissibility

## Definition

Selective admissibility occurs when a system only surfaces evidence, metrics, language, or interpretations that make its preferred story appear coherent.

In TD613 Hush, that failure mode would look like a beautiful mask demo that hides thin samples, dropped evidence, topic leakage, Persona overfit, or context pressure.

## Event-rationality risk

Event-rationality risk appears when a system rationalizes the event it wants: the successful transformation, the flattering score, the clean report, the sealed output. Hush resists that by treating refusal behavior as part of the instrument.

## How Hush resists it

Hush resists selective admissibility by testing hostile cases, preserving limitations, exposing uncertainty, and making refusal behavior part of the release standard.

Specific counters include:

- short sample insufficiency
- topic-matched false authorship contact
- semantic literal failure
- AI-smoothed flattening
- Persona overfit
- glyph / ZWNJ boundary stress
- public context exposure
- Claim Ladder caps
- report forbidden-claim detector

## Where Hush remains vulnerable

Hush can still drift if maintainers tune thresholds to make demos look better, widen fixture ranges without evidence, ignore report limitations, or treat Recognition Field warnings as decorative.

The release discipline should assume scoring drift will happen unless tests keep pressure on the uncomfortable cases.

## Fixture strategy

Fixtures make Hush sweat. They include cases where the right behavior is hold, restore, rotate, lower the claim ceiling, or preserve a warning.

A fixture failure should be inspected before it is softened.

## Claim Ladder strategy

The Claim Ladder limits what reports can say. It is not a confidence trophy. It prevents local metrics from becoming identity claims, platform claims, or publication advice.

## Recognition Field strategy

Recognition Field simulation can show that a text has local source reduction while still remaining hot in context. That tension is valuable. It prevents a single favorable score from taking over the whole review.

## Release discipline

Phase 10 keeps Hush legible to new operators. Documentation, release tests, and responsible-use language make the boundaries visible before the tool is used under pressure.
