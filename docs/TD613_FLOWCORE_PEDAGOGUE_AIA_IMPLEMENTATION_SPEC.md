## 4. Child-legible anisotropic information architecture

### 4.1 Definition

**Child-legible anisotropic information architecture (AIA)** is an information environment whose legibility varies by route, scale, direction, room, registry, role, and prior context while preserving a common custody and claim boundary.

An AIA does not offer one universal simplification. It offers several bounded, mutually accountable entrances into the same governed ecology.

Let the local legibility of a scene depend upon position \(x\), route direction \(v\), scale σ, room \(r\), registry ρ, and operator posture \(p\):

\[
\mathcal L = \mathcal L(x,v,\sigma,r,\rho,p,t).
\]

The anisotropy is intentional. A custodian may need source ancestry. A child may need a visible causal path. An auditor may need evidence status and alternate models. A developer may need exact state and schema references. These surfaces must remain linked without being collapsed.

### 4.2 AIA invariants

Every AIA scene must preserve:

1. **source invariance:** changing presentation does not silently mutate the governed source;
2. **claim invariance:** plain language does not silently widen the technical claim;
3. **missingness visibility:** simplified surfaces do not erase absent or contradictory evidence;
4. **local-section integrity:** room-specific meaning remains attributable to its room;
5. **route traceability:** transformations remain inspectable;
6. **exit parity:** every route preserves a visible stop or return;
7. **technical recoverability:** expert inspection remains available without becoming compulsory;
8. **non-equivalence:** two routes may remain meaningfully different even when they reach similar visible endpoints;
9. **reduced-motion completeness:** motion may aid understanding but may never be the sole carrier of meaning;
10. **no hidden learner profile:** the architecture may adapt to an explicitly selected view, not an inferred cognitive rank.

### 4.3 Progressive legibility

Every major scene should support four coordinated scales.

#### Far scale — world orientation

Answers:

> Where am I, what kind of place is this, and what is currently changing?

#### Middle scale — regional consequence

Answers:

> Which room, route, seam, projection, or custody relation changed?

#### Near scale — causal path

Answers:

> What action produced this change, where did it travel, and what remained unresolved?

#### Technical scale — instrument inspection

Answers:

> Which state, equation, source status, digest, transformation, alternative, and claim ceiling produced the rendering?

The design law is:

> **Legibility is not the elimination of depth. It is the creation of a navigable descent into depth.**

- new prompts stop;
- the latest consequence remains visible;
- pressure decays;
- no score is lost;

- the operator may exit;
